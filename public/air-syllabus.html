Chapter 2 — Principles of Flight
Four Forces of Flight
ForceDirectionCreated By
LiftUpwardWings (aerofoil shape)
Weight (Gravity)DownwardMass of aircraft
ThrustForwardEngine / propeller
DragBackwardAir resistance
For level flight: Lift = Weight and Thrust = Drag
How Wings Generate Lift

Wings have an aerofoil (airfoil) cross-section — curved on top and flatter on bottom. Air moving over the curved top surface travels faster, creating lower pressure. Higher pressure below pushes the wing (and aircraft) upward — this is Bernoulli's Principle.

Chapter 3 — Aircraft Types
Classification by Engine
  • Piston Engine — uses petrol, slower speed, used in light aircraft (e.g. Cessna)
  • Turboprop — jet engine + propeller (e.g. HAL Saras, ATR aircraft)
  • Turbojet — pure jet, high speed (e.g. early jet fighters)
  • Turbofan — most modern jets — fuel efficient (e.g. Boeing 737, Sukhoi Su-30)

Chapter 4 — Aero-modelling
Types of Model Aircraft
  • Free Flight (FF) — no control after launch, flies freely
  • Control Line (CL) — controlled by two wires from flier's hand
  • Radio Control (RC) — controlled by radio transmitter
  • Gliders — no engine, uses air currents
Parts of a Model Aircraft
PartFunction
FuselageMain body of the model
WingGenerates lift
Tailplane (Horizontal Stabiliser)Controls pitch stability
Fin (Vertical Stabiliser)Controls yaw stability
ElevatorControls pitch movement
RudderControls yaw movement
AileronsControls roll movement
Motor/EngineProvides thrust

Standard First Aid — DRABC
  • Danger — check for hazards
  • Response — is casualty conscious?
  • Airway — open and clear airway
  • Breathing — check for breathing
  • Circulation — check pulse, control bleeding
CPR: 30 chest compressions : 2 rescue breaths at 100–120 compressions/minute until help arrives.
